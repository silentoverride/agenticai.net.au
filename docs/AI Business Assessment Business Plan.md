# The Current Model: Annie the Voice Agent

The current version of the assessment is fully automated at the data-gathering stage. Here is how it works:

1. Corey (or whoever sold the assessment) shares a phone number with the business owner.   
2. The owner calls the number at any time, day or night. 'Annie' — an AI voice agent built on Retell.ai — answers immediately.   
3. Annie introduces herself: 'Hi there, I'm Annie from Return My Time. Thanks for hopping on. This is a quick chat to learn about your business and what your day-to-day looks like so I can spot the best places AI can give you time back. You ready to get started?'   
4. Annie asks the owner their name, what their business does, how long they have been running it, how many people are on their team, what tools and software they use, and then — the most important question — 'What's the biggest headache in your workday right now?'   
5. The conversation lasts 20–30 minutes. Annie starts with general questions and moves toward industry-specific ones based on the owner's answers. Corey has built a master question bank from eight or nine different industries, and Annie draws from the relevant questions for each business type.   
6. Annie does not prescribe any solutions or tools on the call. Her only job is to gather information and identify pain points.   
7. When the call ends, the transcript is automatically piped to a separate AI agent that analyzes the conversation and builds the assessment report.

On Camera Demo: Kerner called Annie live during the interview. She greeted him, asked what he does (e-commerce on Amazon), how long he had been doing it (9 years), whether he ran it solo or had a team (2 warehouse employees, 4 VAs), what tools he used (Google Workspace, Smart Scout), and what his biggest bottleneck was (finding new suppliers). The interaction was smooth, natural, and indistinguishable from a human interviewer. Corey's assessment: 'I feel like I'm talking to a human.' She would have continued for another 10-15 minutes identifying more pain points.

# Building the Report: Claude + Presenton

After the transcript is generated, the report-building process is automated in two steps:

# Step 1: Processing the Transcript

A series of Al agent 'skills' (Corey's term for specialized sub-agents, each with one task) processes the raw transcript:

- Skill 1: Clean and format the raw transcript   
- Skill 2: Identify and categorize the pain points mentioned by the owner   
- Skill 3: Research AI and software tools from directories like Futurepedia and There's An AI For That (.com) to find specific tools that address each pain point   
- Skill 4: Map each pain point on an effort vs. impact matrix to prioritize recommendations   
- Skill 5: Calculate the financial impact of recommended changes (hours saved x hourly rate)   
- Output: a structured .docx file containing all assessment content, ready to be formatted

For someone starting out: you do not need this multi-agent architecture. You can take the voice call transcript, paste it into Claude, and say: 'I attached the transcript of a conversation with a local business owner. Your job is to find AI tools or software tools they can implement that are off-the-shelf and can fix the pain points you identified from the transcript.' Claude will do this reliably. Then ask it to explain how to implement each tool.

# Step 2: Report Formatting in Presenton

Once the .docx output is ready, it is uploaded to Presenton (presenton.ai) — an open-source AI presentation generator. Presenton uses the uploaded content to auto-generate a
professionally designed visual report based on a template. The report can be exported as PPTX or PDF. Corey's free template is available at audittemplate.ai.

# The Business Brokerage Case Study: The Knowledge System

A business brokerage owner (Ron) handles 5-7 listings per year, each attracting 200-400 emails in the first week — $95\%$ of which are the same 10 questions:

- Problem: Ron, the owner, is personally answering hundreds of emails about each listing — not the best use of his time for a deal that closes at $500,000+$   
- Solution: a custom GPT trained on the SIM (Selling/Information Memorandum — the marketing package for the listing)

- Economics: once the system prompt template is built, Ron can deploy it for every new listing; the incremental cost is minimal   
- Recurring revenue: charge setup fee for initial build + monthly maintenance/update fee per listing; every new listing is a repeat sale

# Upsell Specialization Strategy

Corey's key advice: do not try to upsell everything from the beginning. Pick one upsell and get very good at it:

- If you are just starting, the Speed-to-Lead agent is the most directly tied to revenue — it is easy to quantify, immediately impactful, and business owners understand it immediately   
- The custom GPT / Knowledge System is the most repeatable — one template, deployed over and over for different clients and different listings/projects   
- Automation builds (Zapier/Make) are the easiest to learn — even basic automation knowledge is worth $1,000-$ 3,000 per implementation   
- Process optimization is the highest ticket but requires more consulting experience — save it for after you have done 10–15 assessments   
- The financial anchor rule: find the upsell that is most directly tied to either revenue generation or cost savings. That is the easiest sell.

# Why This Works Even Without Expertise

The most common objection to starting this business: 'I'm not an AI expert.' Corey's response is definitive: you do not need to be. Here is why:

- Your average client is starting at zero. They are not using Fathom, Fireflies,

DashThis, or any AI tools. They do not know what a custom GPT is. They have never heard of Zapier.

- You only need to be one step ahead — and one step ahead means: you have spent 7 days learning AI tools, you know how to prompt Claude effectively, and you have tested a few tools yourself

- The upsells require more knowledge — but you can start with the simplest upsell (the custom GPT or a basic Zapier automation) and build from there   
- Corey's admission: when the report prescribes a tool he has never heard of, he asks Claude to explain it and how to implement it before the follow-up call. This is normal. The tool does the research.

Identify 20-30 tools across categories: meeting notetakers (Fathom, Fireflies), analytics (DashThis), CRM (GoHighLevel), automation (Zapier, Make.com), knowledge systems (custom GPTs), voice agents (Retell.ai).   
10. Day 4: Watch Corey's Build with AI podcast on YouTube. Study his assessment process and upsell examples. Download the free template at audititemplate.ai and understand how the Presenton report works.   
11. Day 5: Build your first practice assessment. Choose a fictional business (or a real one you know). Write out 10 questions you would ask. Paste them into Claude along with hypothetical answers. Ask Claude to generate an assessment report. Review and refine.   
12. Day 6-7: Set up your tools. Create accounts on Presenton (presenton.ai), Zapier (free tier), and optionally Retell.ai for a voice agent. Set up a simple booking link (Calendly) for assessment discovery calls.

# Week 2: First Outreach and Free Assessments (Days 8-14)

13. Day 8: Identify 3 people in your existing professional network who run businesses. These are your first free assessment targets. Business owners with 5-50 employees and complex day-to-day operations are ideal.   
14. Day 9: Reach out to all three. Message or call. Say: 'I have been spending time learning AI tools and how they apply to small businesses. I would love to do a free 45-minute AI assessment for your business — I'll send you a professional report showing exactly where AI can save you time and money. Zero cost, zero obligation. Want to try it?'   
15. Day 10–12: Conduct your first free assessments over Zoom. Interview each owner using the question framework. Record the call. Take notes on their biggest pain points. After each call, paste the notes or transcript into Claude and generate the report draft. Format in Gamma.   
16. Day 13: Deliver the first report to your first free client. Schedule a 30-minute follow-up call to walk through it. Pay close attention to their reaction to each recommendation — this tells you what resonates.   
17. Day 14: Follow-up calls for all three free assessments. At the end of each: 'I noticed [specific pain point] could benefit from a more involved solution. I can build [specific

automation/custom GPT/agent] for you for $\$ 1$ price]. Want to explore that?' Even one yes at this stage validates the entire model.

# Week 3: First Paid Assessment and Client Acquisition (Days 15-21)

18. Day 15: Set up your LinkedIn profile if not already active. Write one post about what you learned from your free assessment experience. Be specific and honest. This is the beginning of your content presence.   
19. Day 16: Identify a local professional office (real estate office, insurance agency, accounting firm) and propose an AI office hours session. You show up once or twice a month; answer anyone's AI questions; no pitch. Your goal is contacts and leads.   
20. Day 17–18: Start charging. Your first paid assessment should be at $200–$500 as you are still refining your process. Do not start at $1,000 until you have delivered five to ten assessments. Reach out to warm contacts and charge the assessment fee.   
21. Day 19–20: Research local CEO groups, peer networks, or professional associations you could join or present at. Apply to speak at one. Prepare a 20-minute 'Al for Small Business' presentation — pure value, no pitch.   
22. Day 21: Reflect and document. What questions worked best in your assessments? What tools got the most excited reactions? What upsell opportunities came up most often? Update your question bank and tool recommendations based on real data.

# Week 4: Scale and Systematize (Days 22-30)

23. Day 22- 24: Build your first Retell.ai voice agent if you are technically inclined. Otherwise, continue doing Zoom intake calls. The voice agent can come later — the business works without it.   
24. Day 25: Raise your price to $500 if you started at $200. Raise to $1,000 once you have 10 completed assessments and at least 3 testimonials.   
25. Day 26-28: Systematize the follow-up process. Every assessment should end with a booking link for the follow-up call. Every follow-up call should end with a clear upsell proposal or a 'not now' categorization. Build a simple CRM (a spreadsheet is fine at this stage) to track every prospect.   
26. Day 29-30: Pick your first upsell specialty. Based on your first 10 assessments, which upsell came up most often? Custom GPT? Zapier automation? Speed-to-lead agent? Get very good at that one thing. This is how you productize the upsell and increase your effective hourly rate.
